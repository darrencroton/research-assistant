# Arxiv Priorities

## Categories
- astro-ph.GA
- astro-ph.CO

## Priorities - Science
1. Galaxy formation and evolution at all epochs, with emphasis on z > 2: stellar mass assembly, star formation histories, morphological transformation, quenching and feedback, cosmic star formation rate density, JWST-era early galaxy assembly, cosmic dawn, reionization-era galaxies, little red dots (LRDs), and compact red JWST sources
2. Black holes and AGN in the context of galaxy evolution: SMBH growth and co-evolution with host galaxies, AGN feedback including outflows, jets, and quenching, AGN triggering via mergers and secular processes, AGN demographics and duty cycles, high-z and JWST AGN, and LRDs as candidate AGN or obscured quasars
3. The galaxy-halo connection: stellar-to-halo mass relation, abundance matching (SHAM), halo occupation distribution (HOD), galaxy bias, assembly bias, and links between galaxy properties and dark matter halo properties or merger histories
4. Galaxy environments and their effects on galaxy evolution: groups, clusters, filaments, voids, the cosmic web, environmental quenching, ram pressure stripping, tidal effects, pre-processing, galaxy-galaxy interactions, mergers, and close pairs
5. Intracluster and intragroup light: ICL, IGL, diffuse stellar envelopes, formation and build-up of diffuse light in groups and clusters, ICL stellar populations, and ICL as a tracer of group and cluster assembly history

## Priorities - Methods
1. Semi-analytic and semi-empirical galaxy formation models: semi-analytic models (SAMs), SAGE, L-Galaxies, SHARK, Santa Cruz SAM and similar frameworks, abundance matching (SHAM), stellar-to-halo mass modelling, and halo occupation distribution (HOD) modelling and fitting
2. Cosmological N-body and hydrodynamical simulations: large-volume dark matter simulations, merger trees, subhalo evolution, halo catalogues, large-volume hydrodynamical simulations such as IllustrisTNG, EAGLE, and SIMBA, zoom-in simulations of galaxies, groups, and clusters, and mock catalogues or synthetic observations for comparison with data
3. Galaxy surveys as benchmarks and targets for theoretical models: deep and wide photometric surveys, spectroscopic redshift surveys, IFU and integral-field surveys, multiwavelength surveys from radio to X-ray, and survey datasets that motivate, calibrate, or test models of galaxy formation and evolution
4. Statistical tools for galaxy clustering and large-scale structure: two-point correlation functions (2PCF), projected and angular correlation functions, power spectra, cross-correlations, marked statistics, and halo occupation analysis used to probe the galaxy-halo connection, environment, and large-scale structure
